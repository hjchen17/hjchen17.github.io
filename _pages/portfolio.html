Breaking the Wall of High Loss in Photonic Chips

Towards fibre-like loss photonic integrated circuits from violet to near-infrared

Optical fiber built the global internet because of its exceptionally low light transmission loss. Modern computing was enabled by scalable integrated circuits. This project brings these two technological foundations together by adapting fiber-optic glass to a CMOS-foundry-compatible photonic platform, breaking the longstanding loss wall in integrated photonics and enabling a new generation of coherent, energy-efficient, and deployable photonic systems.

Core idea

Every lost photon on a chip means more laser power, more heat, less coherence, and less deployable photonic systems. This project attacks that bottleneck at its source by adapting fiber-derived germanium-doped silica to a CMOS-compatible process and using wafer-scale reflow to smooth waveguides to atomic-scale roughness.

Why low loss matters

Integrated photonics has long faced a “loss wall”: microfabrication-induced roughness scatters light, especially at visible wavelengths, increasing power consumption, heat, and noise while reducing coherence. Lower loss is not just a better device metric — it is the difference between a laboratory demonstration and a deployable photonic system.

Lower power

Every lost photon demands more laser power. Ultralow-loss chips can reduce the power overhead of optical links and coherent photonic systems.

Lower heat and noise

Loss translates into heating, instability, and degraded coherence. Reducing scattering directly improves precision and scalability.

More deployable systems

Low-loss, compact, and manufacturable photonics can move precision technologies out of the lab and into real-world use.

From optical fiber and integrated circuits to a new photonic platform

The internet was built on low-loss optical fiber, while modern computing was built on scalable integrated circuits. My project combines these two foundations by bringing the material logic of fiber into manufacturable photonic chips.

Concept figure: low-loss optical fiber and large-scale integrated circuits together motivate a new generation of fibre-like photonic integrated circuits.

The technical breakthrough

Instead of optimizing one photonic device at a time, this work creates a new photonic platform. The key invention is a wafer-scale self-smoothing process: higher GeO2 doping than in standard fiber lowers the glass softening temperature, enabling reflow in a foundry-compatible furnace. Surface tension then smooths rough waveguides to atomic-scale roughness, removing scattering loss at its source.

  • Fiber-derived germanosilica adapted to a CMOS-foundry-compatible process
  • Wafer-scale reflow that smooths waveguides to atomic-scale roughness
  • Direct improvement in coherence for visible-to-infrared photonics
  • A shared platform for lasers, Brillouin devices, and dispersion-engineered microcombs

Why this is a paradigm shift

Old tradeoff

Fiber delivers ultralow loss, while chips deliver scalable integration. Integrated photonics has long been forced to accept this compromise.

New possibility

This project shows that fiber-derived glass can deliver ultralow loss in scalable chips, removing the old tradeoff between coherence and manufacturability.

From device to platform

The result is not just a better resonator or a better waveguide, but a new foundation for visible photonics, coherent lasers, microcombs, and integrated precision systems.

What this platform enables

AI optical interconnects

Lower-loss photonic chips can cut laser power, heat, and electricity use in optical interconnects, supporting more sustainable data-center growth.

Precision timing & navigation

Higher coherence and compactness can shrink precision timing and navigation systems for space exploration and GPS-denied environments.

Large-scale quantum systems

Over the longer term, ultralow-loss photonic platforms can support large-scale quantum photonic systems for discovery in chemistry, materials, and medicine.
